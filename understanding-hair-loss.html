The Science of Hair Growth

Hair growth follows a cyclical process with three distinct phases:

  • Anagen (Growth Phase): Hair grows actively from the follicle. This phase lasts 2–6 years.
  • Catagen (Transition Phase): Hair growth halts, and the follicle begins to shrink. This phase lasts 2–3 weeks.
  • Telogen (Resting Phase): Hair rests for 2–4 months before shedding.

Disruptions in this cycle, whether due to genetic, hormonal, or environmental factors, lead to hair loss.

1. Genetic Factors: The Role of Androgenetic Alopecia (AGA)

Androgenetic alopecia, commonly known as male or female pattern baldness, is the most prevalent form of hair loss. It affects:

  • 50% of men and 25% of women by age 50

How Genetics Drive Hair Loss

  • AGA is polygenic, meaning multiple genes influence its development. These genes are inherited from both maternal and paternal lineages.
  • A key gene is the AR (Androgen Receptor) gene, located on the X chromosome. It increases sensitivity to androgens like dihydrotestosterone (DHT).

The Role of DHT

Dihydrotestosterone (DHT) is a derivative of testosterone, formed by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase. It binds to androgen receptors in hair follicles, shrinking them in a process called follicular miniaturization.

2. Hormonal Imbalances

Hair follicles are highly sensitive to hormonal fluctuations, which can disrupt the hair growth cycle.

Postpartum Hair Loss

After childbirth, estrogen levels drop sharply, leading to temporary shedding, known as telogen effluvium.

Thyroid Disorders

Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism disrupt metabolic processes, impairing follicle function.

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)

Women with PCOS have elevated androgen levels, leading to male-pattern hair thinning on the scalp.

3. Autoimmune and Medical Conditions

Autoimmune disorders and medical conditions can significantly impact hair health:

Alopecia Areata

An autoimmune disorder in which the immune system attacks hair follicles, causing patchy hair loss.

Scarring Alopecia

Diseases like lichen planopilaris destroy hair follicles, leading to irreversible hair loss.

Systemic Illnesses

Chronic conditions like diabetes can interfere with nutrient delivery to hair follicles.

4. Nutritional Deficiencies

Key nutrients like iron, protein, and vitamin D are vital for healthy hair:

Iron Deficiency

Iron is essential for hemoglobin production, which delivers oxygen to hair follicles.

Protein Deficiency

Lack of dietary protein can halt hair growth, causing diffuse hair loss.

Vitamin D Deficiency

Vitamin D regulates hair follicle cycling, and deficiencies can lead to stunted growth.

5. Stress: The Trigger for Telogen Effluvium

Both acute and chronic stress can disrupt the hair growth cycle:

Acute Stress

Emotional or physical stress can lead to telogen effluvium, where many hairs prematurely enter the telogen phase.

Chronic Stress

Long-term stress elevates cortisol levels, impairing follicle function and reducing hair growth.

6. Environmental and Lifestyle Factors

Lifestyle choices and environmental exposures can damage hair health:

Hairstyling and Mechanical Damage

Tight hairstyles and heat styling tools can cause traction alopecia and breakage.

Pollution

Particulate matter induces oxidative stress, leading to follicle damage.

Smoking and Alcohol

Smoking reduces blood flow to the scalp, and heavy alcohol consumption exacerbates nutrient deficiencies.

7. Medications and Treatments

Certain medications and treatments can cause hair loss:

Drugs That Cause Hair Loss

Common culprits include chemotherapy drugs, beta-blockers, and retinoids.
